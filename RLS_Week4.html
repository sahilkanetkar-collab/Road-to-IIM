The Core Distinction

States vs Implies

There are two types of questions in the exam room that look nearly identical but require completely different operations from you.

"According to the passage..." -- this is a retrieval question. The answer is a paraphrase of something the author wrote explicitly. You find it, you match it, you move on.

"It can be inferred from the passage that..." -- this is an inference question. The answer is something the author did not write. It is the next step in the argument: the only conclusion the passage logically permits.

Confusing these two is one of the most common and costly mistakes in RC. The student who tries to retrieve the answer to an inference question reads every option looking for something the passage said -- and finds nothing, or picks a close paraphrase that is still wrong.

Adler's Framework -- Ch. 9

Propositions and Arguments

Adler draws a precise distinction between words, propositions, and arguments. Words are units of language. Propositions are what the author is asserting -- a claim about the world that can be true or false. Arguments are chains of propositions leading to a conclusion.

Adler's Law

Every sentence in an RC passage either states a fact, makes a claim, or supports a conclusion. Knowing which is which tells you what the author is permitted to conclude -- and what counts as a valid inference from the passage.

A proposition is not just what the author says. It is what the author is committing to. If the author writes "studies show that X leads to Y," the author is asserting that relationship as true. The inference the passage permits is: wherever X is present, Y is more likely. That is one step. Two steps away: where X is absent, Y may not occur. Three steps away: Y is caused only by X. That third step is not permitted -- it is beyond what the proposition commits to.

The Exam-Facing Skill

Logical Distance

Every inference question has a correct answer and at least one trap that is too far from the passage. The trap feels logical -- it sounds like a reasonable conclusion -- but it requires a step the passage never authorised.

The discipline is measuring logical distance: how many steps does it take to get from what the passage said to what the option claims?

0

Stated -- not an inference question

The author writes this explicitly. Retrieval, not inference. The correct answer to an inference question will never be a direct quote.

1

One step -- valid inference

The only conclusion the stated proposition logically permits. The passage does not say it, but it commits to it. This is almost always the correct answer.

2

Two steps -- usually wrong

Requires combining the passage's proposition with an assumption the passage never made. Feels reasonable. Often the most attractive trap.

3+

Too far -- always wrong

A conclusion that sounds logical but requires multiple unstated assumptions. The passage cannot support it even if the conclusion is true in the real world.

The Classic Trap

"The Author Implies" vs "The Author States"

How the trap is set

Passage says: "Students who slept fewer than six hours before the test scored an average of 18% lower."

Inference question option: "Sleep deprivation is the primary cause of poor exam performance."

This is a two-step trap. The passage asserts a correlation. The option claims causation and primacy -- two assumptions the passage never made. The one-step inference would be: "Students who slept fewer than six hours tended to perform worse on the test." That is all the proposition permits.

The right answer to an inference question is usually the one that sounds almost too cautious -- the one that feels like it barely adds anything to what the passage said. That restraint is the signal. A valid inference is the smallest step the evidence permits.

IPMAT Indore -- The Payoff

Why This Week Matters Most

IPMAT Indore is the most inference-heavy of all the exams you are preparing for. A typical IPMAT Indore RC section will have three to four inference questions per passage -- questions that explicitly ask what can be concluded, inferred, or implied.

The students who score in the top band on these questions are not the ones who know more vocabulary or have read more books. They are the ones who have trained themselves to measure logical distance precisely -- to stop exactly one step from the passage and not take a single step further without authorisation.

The Two-Question Test

Before selecting an inference answer, ask:

1. Does the passage actually say this? If yes, it is retrieval, not inference -- wrong answer type for the question.

2. Does getting from the passage to this answer require an assumption the passage never made? If yes, it is too far -- wrong answer for the question.

The Drumbeat

"An inference is not a guess. It is the only step the passage permits."

This Week's Takeaway

"One step from the text. No more."

Three Inference Points

Read the passage below fully. Three points have been annotated. Each reveal shows you what the passage is committing to at that moment -- and what a valid inference from that proposition looks like.

Passage -- Argumentative · Productivity Science

The widespread belief that multitasking improves productivity is not merely mistaken -- it is measurably costly. Every time attention shifts from one task to another, the brain incurs what researchers call a "switch cost": a brief but real period of reduced efficiency as the prefrontal cortex reassigns its priorities. When task-switching happens repeatedly across a working day, these costs accumulate into significant losses of both speed and accuracy.

Inference Point 1 -- "accumulate into significant losses"

What the passage states: switch costs are real, and they accumulate across a working day.

One-step inference: A person who multitasks throughout the day will produce meaningfully worse output -- in speed and accuracy -- than a person who does not.

Two-step trap: Multitasking is the single biggest cause of workplace inefficiency. (The passage does not compare multitasking to other causes. It never establishes primacy -- only that the costs are "significant.")

The appeal of multitasking is, in part, a confusion between two very different things: doing several things in sequence and doing several things simultaneously. The former is simply efficient scheduling; the latter is neurologically impossible for cognitively demanding tasks. What people call multitasking is almost always rapid sequential switching -- and the evidence consistently shows that people who believe themselves to be skilled multitaskers are, on measurable cognitive tests, the worst performers of all.

Inference Point 2 -- "worst performers of all"

What the passage states: those who believe they are skilled multitaskers score lowest on cognitive performance tests.

One-step inference: Confidence in one's multitasking ability is not a reliable signal of actual multitasking performance -- in fact, it is inversely correlated with it.

Two-step trap: Multitaskers have worse metacognitive ability than non-multitaskers in general. (The passage is only about self-assessed multitasking skill, not metacognition as a broad category.)

This matters beyond individual productivity. Organisations that celebrate multitasking as a competency signal a misunderstanding of how knowledge work actually functions. The capacity for sustained, uninterrupted attention -- what the researcher Cal Newport calls "deep work" -- is not a luxury for an elite minority. It is the mechanism by which complex problems are solved, and it is directly undermined by a culture that treats constant task-switching as a mark of capability.

Inference Point 3 -- "organisations that celebrate multitasking"

What the passage states: organisations that reward multitasking misunderstand knowledge work; deep work is the mechanism for solving complex problems; a multitasking culture undermines this mechanism.

One-step inference (and the skeleton): Organisations that value multitasking are actively reducing their capacity to solve complex problems. The whole argument arrives here -- this is where the skeleton sits.

Skeleton: "Multitasking is measurably costly because switch costs accumulate, and organisations that reward it are undermining their own ability to do complex work."

Drill Passage -- Argumentative · Education Policy

The use of standardised test scores to evaluate teacher quality rests on an assumption so rarely examined that most education systems have simply built it into policy: that what students learn in a given year is primarily a function of what their teacher does in that year. This assumption is not only empirically questionable -- it is, in the cases where it has been tested, largely false.

Student achievement is shaped by an accumulation of prior learning, home environment, peer effects, and nutrition, among other factors. A teacher who inherits a class with two years of instructional deficit is not being assessed on her own performance when her students score below average -- she is being assessed on a history she did not write. Conversely, a teacher whose students arrive already high-achieving may produce impressive test results while contributing relatively little to their growth.

The defenders of test-score evaluation will argue that "value-added" models, which measure individual student growth rather than absolute scores, correct for these distortions. They do not. Value-added scores are statistically volatile: the same teacher, with similar students, can score in the top quartile one year and the bottom quartile the next. A measure that unstable is not measuring teaching -- it is measuring noise.

What is actually being defended, in most cases, is not accurate evaluation but cheap evaluation. Standardised scores are inexpensive to collect and easy to rank. The convenience of the metric has been mistaken for its validity.
