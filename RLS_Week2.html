Week 2 · The Lesson

Your First Job Is Classification

Last week you learned that the demanding reader asks four questions of every passage. But here is the thing no one tells you: you cannot answer those questions in order if you don't know what kind of passage you're reading first.

The first pass is a fast, purposeful sweep whose only goal is to classify and orient. Not to understand everything. Not to annotate. Not to answer questions. Just to find the floor plan before you move in.

Every passage has a skeleton. The first pass finds it. The answers follow.

Two Moves on the First Pass

The first pass has two moves that happen in quick succession. Think of them as two gears, not two separate sessions.

Move 1 — Systematic Skimming

Read the first sentence of each paragraph. Read the last paragraph in full. Look for any sentence that seems to be summing up or pivoting. You are not reading the passage — you are reading its structure. After 60 seconds of this, you should know: what is this about, and where is the argument going?

Move 2 — Superficial Reading

Now read the passage through — once, without stopping. The rule is blunt: "Read it through without ever stopping to look up or ponder the things you do not understand right away." Mark your Three Alerts as you go, but do not stop for them. The flag is not a roadblock. Forward momentum is the point.

Classifying the Passage

The single most important thing the first pass tells you is what kind of passage you are reading. In RC, this almost always comes down to one question: is the author stacking information, or pushing a position?

Think of it this way — some texts teach you that something is true. Others teach you what you should do or believe about it. You can feel the difference in the language.

⚔️ Argumentative 📚 Additive
Author is pushing a position. Signal words: however but yet despite should ought must contrary to Author is stacking information. Signal words: furthermore in addition also another similarly
Skeleton sits at the end. The passage builds toward a claim. Read the last paragraph first on your skim. Skeleton sits near the start. The passage announces a topic and elaborates. The first paragraph usually gives it to you.
Tone markers: criticism, concern, urgency, counter-argument. The author disagrees with someone. Tone markers: neutral, explanatory, survey-like. The author is informing, not debating.

The Skeleton Rule

The rule is simple and ruthless: "State the unity of the whole passage in a single sentence." And there's no softening it — "Do not be satisfied with 'feeling the unity' that you cannot express. The reader who says, 'I know what it is, but I just can't say it,' probably does not even fool himself."

This is your skeleton. Not a summary. Not a list of topics. One sentence that captures what the author is doing — and why. Until you can write it, you have not finished the first pass.

You cannot find the skeleton by stopping at every brick. Read the whole building first.

The Three Alerts on the First Pass

🔴 Red Alert — Unknown Word

Mark it. Keep moving. You will come back with context. Stopping now gives you the worst possible position for inference — you haven't read what comes next yet.

🟡 Yellow Alert — Reread Sentence

Mark it. Keep moving. A confusing sentence is almost always clarified two sentences later. Trust the passage.

🟠 Orange Alert — Paragraph Disconnect

This is your most important alert on the first pass. A paragraph that shifts direction unexpectedly is usually the hinge of the argument — the pivot where the author's position becomes clear. Mark it, and pay attention to the sentence that follows.

Why This Changes Everything

When you know the passage type before you answer, you eliminate half the trap options instantly. A main-idea question on an argumentative passage will always have one option that states the topic — not the argument. Students who skipped the first pass fall for it every time. The student who classified first knows the answer must capture the position, not just the subject.

This Week's Takeaway

The first pass is not slow reading. It is a fast sweep for structure. You are looking for the floor plan, not the furniture. Thirty seconds of skimming before you read properly will save you two minutes of confusion after.

"Every passage has a skeleton. The first pass finds it. The answers follow."

Week 2 · Worked Example

Applying the First Pass

Read the passage below using the two-move approach: skim the topic sentences first (30 seconds), then read through without stopping. Work through the annotations after.

Sleep has long been treated as the passive counterpart to wakefulness — a period of biological rest during which little of consequence occurs. Neuroscientific research over the past two decades has overturned this view almost entirely. Far from being a state of suspension, sleep is now understood to be a period of intense and highly organised neural activity.

The most significant discoveries concern the role of sleep in memory consolidation. During slow-wave sleep, the hippocampus replays sequences of neural activity that occurred during waking hours, effectively transferring newly acquired information into longer-term cortical storage. During REM sleep, the brain appears to integrate this information with existing knowledge structures, making connections that were not apparent during waking experience.

A second function concerns the clearance of metabolic waste. The glymphatic system — a network of fluid-filled channels surrounding blood vessels in the brain — operates primarily during sleep, flushing out proteins such as beta-amyloid that accumulate during neural activity. Disrupted sleep has been associated with elevated beta-amyloid levels, a marker strongly linked to the development of Alzheimer's disease.

What emerges from this research is a picture of sleep not as absence but as architecture. The body is still; the brain is not.

~195 words

Work through the annotations

Systematic Skimming Topic sentence P1: "Sleep has long been treated as passive…" — a prior view being set up. Topic sentence P2: memory consolidation. Topic sentence P3: clearance of metabolic waste. Final paragraph: "sleep not as absence but as architecture." Three cues: the opening overturns a prior view, the middle paragraphs stack evidence, the last delivers a conclusion. This looks additive — the author is building a cumulative picture.
Classification — Additive Additive. The prior view ("sleep is passive") is dismissed in sentence one. Everything that follows adds to a picture of sleep's active functions: memory consolidation, glymphatic clearance, integrating metaphor. Signal words throughout are additive: a second function, what emerges. No counter-argument appears. Skeleton sits near the start — confirmed by P1's topic sentence.
Orange Alert — Paragraph Disconnect The only potential disconnect is P1 → P2. P1 is about perception; P2 suddenly introduces hippocampal replay and cortical storage. This is not a contradiction — it is the first piece of evidence for the overturned view. The Orange Alert here signals a shift from framing to evidence. Once you recognise this as additive, the structure resolves immediately.
Red Alert — Unknown Term Glymphatic system is a technical term most readers won't know. But the passage immediately defines it: "a network of fluid-filled channels surrounding blood vessels in the brain." This is the pattern — mark the Red Alert, keep reading, and the passage gives you what you need within one sentence. The passage was the dictionary.
Model Skeleton "Sleep is not passive rest but a period of organised neural activity, serving critical functions in memory consolidation and brain waste clearance."

One sentence, captures the what (organised neural activity) and the why it matters (memory, waste clearance). A student who wrote this before answering questions would have eliminated every "topic-only" wrong option instantly.

For most of the twentieth century, urban planners treated the private car as the natural endpoint of city design. Roads were widened, rail lines removed, and entire neighbourhoods demolished to make way for expressways. The logic seemed unassailable: more roads would reduce congestion by giving vehicles more space to move.

The evidence, however, has consistently told a different story. Traffic engineers now recognise the phenomenon of induced demand — the observation that new road capacity does not relieve congestion but generates additional traffic to fill it. When Seoul demolished its elevated Cheonggyecheon expressway in 2005, traffic modellers predicted gridlock. Instead, congestion in the surrounding area fell. When San Francisco's Embarcadero Freeway collapsed in an earthquake and was later removed, the traffic it had carried simply vanished. Drivers adjusted their routes, their timings, and their modes.

These cases are not anomalies. A comprehensive review of road expansion projects across major cities found that a ten percent increase in lane kilometres produces, on average, a ten percent increase in vehicle kilometres travelled within a decade — effectively erasing the intended benefit. The road builds the traffic that justifies the road.

The implication is uncomfortable for planners who have spent careers expanding road networks: the only reliable way to reduce car traffic in cities is to reduce the space and convenience available to cars. Investment in driving capacity is, paradoxically, an investment in more driving.

~240 words

Q2The author's primary purpose in mentioning Seoul and San Francisco is to —

A misreads — the San Francisco earthquake is incidental; the passage concerns what happened after removal. B is correct: both cities are offered as evidence that removal relieved, not worsened, congestion. C introduces environmental arguments the passage never makes. D is partially true but too narrow — commuter adaptability is a mechanism, not the primary purpose of the examples.

Q3Which of the following can be inferred from the passage's discussion of induced demand?

A is correct: a ten percent increase in lanes produces a ten percent increase in vehicle kilometres within a decade — the benefit is erased. This is a direct, permitted inference. B contradicts the passage — induced demand is a general phenomenon. C cannot be inferred — the passage says engineers "now recognise" it, not that they previously dismissed it. D goes beyond the passage — public transport is never mentioned.

Q4The phrase "the road builds the traffic that justifies the road" is best understood as —

A introduces a blame-angle absent from the passage. B misreads "justifies" as physical maintenance rather than policy rationale. C contradicts the passage's position. D is correct: the phrase is a compact, ironic summary of the induced demand cycle — new roads create new traffic, which then appears to prove that the road was needed.

Q5The author's tone in the final paragraph is best described as —

The final paragraph calls the implication "uncomfortable" and presents it plainly as a paradox. A would require expressions of understanding or forgiveness — none appear. B is correct: the author acknowledges the awkwardness but doesn't soften it; the conclusion follows from the evidence without emotional charge. C would require language of impatience. D would require forward-looking, hopeful language — the passage ends on a paradox, not a prediction.

Q6When the Cheonggyecheon expressway was demolished, traffic engineers predicted gridlock. What does the passage imply about this prediction?

A contradicts the passage — induced demand is precisely why the prediction was wrong. B imputes motive the passage does not support. C is correct: the prediction assumed traffic was fixed. The passage shows the opposite — traffic is elastic; drivers "adjusted their routes, timings, and modes." D contradicts the passage directly — congestion fell, and no subsequent reversal is mentioned.
