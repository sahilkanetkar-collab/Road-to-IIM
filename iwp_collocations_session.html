Why Words Demand Specific Prepositions
Semantic logic, not rote memory — understand the pull behind each family
The word already knows what comes next.

English prepositions are not random. When a word carries a deep sense — fullness, lack, resistance, immersion — it pulls a specific preposition with it. Brimming wants something overflowing and good; it demands with. Devoid describes total absence; it demands of. Mired suggests sinking into something bad; it demands in.

This means you can infer both the preposition and the valence (positive/negative/neutral) from the word itself. That inference is the tool this session builds.

WITH — Fullness & Affliction

The container is full. Valence is locked: brimming wants good things; riddled and rife want bad ones. The preposition follows from the idea of being filled.

OF — Emptiness & Composition

Two senses: lacking (devoid, bereft) and standing-for (emblematic, symptomatic). Bereft = lost what it had. Devoid = never had it. A subtle but testable distinction.

TO — Resistance & Susceptibility

Things that resist or are vulnerable to something else. The wall blocks: impervious to, immune to. The weak point yields: susceptible to, prone to. Direction is the key idea.

IN — Immersion & Entanglement

You are inside something. Positive immersion: versed in, schooled in. Negative entanglement: mired in, embroiled in. The valence of the immersion changes; the preposition stays.

FOR — Reputation & Suitability

What you are known or suited for. Reputation splits by valence: renowned = positive; notorious = negative. Both demand for.

FROM — Separation & Origin

Distance or derivation. You come from something or you are separated from it. Exam trap: different from, not "different than."

ON — Dependence & Focus

Resting upon or contingent upon. Predicated on, contingent on, centred on. Exam trap: centred on, not "centred around."

AT / OVER — Skill & Emotion

Skill targets: adept at, proficient at. Emotional reactions take at or over depending on whether the trigger is immediate (aghast at) or lingering (agonise over).

AGAINST / INTO — Opposition & Change

Forces working against something or transformations into something else. Biased against, rail against; coerced into, lapse into.

The Valence Shortcut

When you see a collocation, the valence chip tells you what kind of thing usually follows. This is a fast inference tool in RC and Verbal:

▼ Negative Valence is locked — the word pulls bad objects. Fraught with danger, never fraught with joy.
▲ Positive Locked positive. Brimming with confidence, not brimming with despair.
● Neutral Context-dependent. Laden with can take guilt (bad) or meaning (neutral).

⚠ Five Exam Traps — Drill These Until They're Automatic

Trap 1
Write: averse to  |  Never: averse from
"She is averse to risk" — the preposition TO is locked. There is no 'averse from' in standard English.
Trap 2
Write: different from  |  Never: different than
"This is different from the others" — exams test this every cycle. 'Different than' is American informal; avoid it.
Trap 3
Write: centred on  |  Never: centred around
"The debate was centred on fiscal policy" — a centre is a point, not an area. You centre ON something, not around it.
Trap 4
Write: oblivious to or oblivious of  |  Both are valid
"He was oblivious to the danger" — TO is more common in modern usage; OF is traditional. Either is accepted, but TO is the safer exam choice.
Trap 5
Write: partial to  |  Never: partial toward
"She is partial to sweets" — partial means having a preference for, and that preference is directed TO, not toward.
