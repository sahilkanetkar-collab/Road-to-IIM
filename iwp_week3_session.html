The Roots of Age
Norman Lewis builds a whole vocabulary of life stages from four number roots. Once you know them, you'll never confuse a septuagenarian with a nonagenarian again.
If someone is in their "eighth decade," how old are they?
Think: the first decade is 0–9, the second is 10–19, so the eighth is... 70–79? Or 80–89? The Latin root will settle it.
Root 1 — OCT / OCTO (Latin & Greek) = eight
octogenarian
noun
A person in their eighties — aged 80 to 89. Built on Latin octoginta (eighty).
"Many octogenarians today live active, independent lives."
octagon
noun
An eight-sided / eight-angled geometric figure. From octo (eight) + Greek gonia (angle).
"A stop sign is octagonal — eight sides, unmistakable at a glance."
octet
noun
A musical group of eight, or a composition written for eight performers.
"Schubert's Octet in F major is one of his most celebrated chamber works."
Root 2 — DEC / DEKA (Latin & Greek) = ten
decade
noun
A period of ten years. From Greek deka (ten).
"The decade of the 1960s was marked by social upheaval and nonconformism."
decagon
noun
A ten-sided geometric figure. From Greek deka (ten) + gonia (angle).
"A decagon has ten angles and ten sides — double the pentagon."
pentagon
noun
A five-sided figure. From Greek penta (five) + gonia (angle). Shares the -gon root with octagon and decagon.
"The Pentagon got its name because the building has five equal sides."
Root 3 — NONA (Latin nonaginta) = ninety
nonagenarian
noun
A person in their nineties — aged 90 to 99. Built on Latin nonaginta (ninety).
"The nonagenarian still completed the morning crossword every day without help."
centenarian
noun
A person who has reached 100 years of age. From Latin centum (100). Completes the age bracket series.
"When she turned 100, local papers ran a story about the new centenarian in town."
Root 4 — SEPTU (Latin septuaginta) = seventy
septuagenarian
noun
A person in their seventies — aged 70 to 79. Built on Latin septuaginta (seventy).
"She retired as a septuagenarian and promptly enrolled in a painting class."
The full age bracket sequence:
70s → septuagenarian  |  80s → octogenarian  |  90s → nonagenarian  |  100 → centenarian

At 76, she refused to be called old. "I'm a ___________," she insisted, "not an octogenarian yet."
Root: septu (seventy) + -agenarian
